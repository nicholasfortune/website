DRAGONFLY

Background

24ᵗʰ of May, 2025

This January, I started tinkering with my first real engineering project: a guided "anti-drone" missile. I'd made model rockets and RC planes and other things over the years, but nothing of this caliber.

Here are some legal questions:
Am I really going to hit a drone with it? No, that's irresponsible. Is it even capable of hitting a drone? No. The GPS is very imprecise, and it has no way of sensing the drone, either. It is given a coordinate before launch, and tries to get as close as possible to that coordinate.

So don't start hiding your drones, because the skies are safe for now.

Oh, yeah. And I haven't even launched this thing yet.

Designing

The missile is made of four sections:
  • The engine block
  • The raceway
  • The flight computer
  • The recovery block
All of which I made up as I designed the missile in Fusion 360. The designing process took 2 weeks, but it felt like a few days. This was the best part of the project, but we still have 13 more weeks to go until the last time I worked on it. So what happens next?

Flight Computer

The close second for my favorite part of the project. In the summer of 2024 I started watching The 8-Bit Guy and Ben Eater and decided I wanted to make a breadboard 6502 computer. I read datasheets, read reddit threads until my eyes went blurry for good, and ultimately got more familiar with breadboards than I'd like to be, so this was right up my alley. I started with an Arduino Mega as the processor because of it's extra serial ports and memory, and from there I used:
  • A NEO-6M based GPS module from Amazon
  • MPU6050 as the gyroscope
  • IRLZ34N MOSFETS for power-cycling the gps
  • Some unnamed LEDs and buzzers

Programming

I got started testing the GPS first because I'd never played with anything like it. It was magical- up until I needed to increase the GPS sample rate. I spent 3, long, grueling weeks trying everything I could think of, until I figured it out! If you go to the u-blox website, go to an entirely different GPS module, find the datasheet and scroll through 174 pages of datasheet hell until you find the CFG-RATE command, and then encode it with the 8-bit fletcher's checksum algorithm, you will get this:

0xB5, 0x62, 0x06, 0x08, 0x06, 0x00, 0xC8, 0x00, 0x01, 0x00, 0x00, 0x00, 0xDD, 0x68

That is the culmination of 3 weeks' work. Amazing.

But what's even more amazing is what it allows us to do. This command tells the GPS to try to change the GPS sample rate from 1hz to 5hz, which is still bad for a mach 0.4 missile, but it's 5 times better than what we had before, so it's a W.

But once again, the GPS decided it needed to ruin my progress even more, so it started noising up all of the data, making an ACK response rare and successful boot near impossible! I tried everything! Shortening the data wires- actually, that's all I tried. So I had a few options, I could:
  1. Shorten the wire even more to reduce noise
  2. Get a better GPS module with a better checksum algorithm
  3. Brute force the command 10 times
If you guessed "c" give yourself a gold star!

The computer will then also do some math for the fins (not as interesting as the above math, though) to figure out where the fins should be pointed to make our real direction into our target direction, which will then be sent off to the fins to make them move in that direction.
